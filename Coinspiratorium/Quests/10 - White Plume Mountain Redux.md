# "The Ghost of White Plume Mountain (Yes, Again)"

> *"Dungeon design is an art. You gotta know your predecessors' failures."* — [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]

---

## Adventure Summary
[[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] sends the PCs to White Plume Mountain — one of the most famous dungeons in existence — during its dormant cycle to steal the [[Keraptis]] Binder, a tome containing every rejected trap design that didn't make the final dungeon. The mountain sleeps. The main traps are powered down. The legendary weapons (Blackrazor, Wave, Whelm) are dormant in their display chambers. It should be a cakewalk.

It isn't. Because the rejected traps — the designs [[Keraptis]] discarded for being too weird, too broken, or too embarrassing — have been waiting centuries for their moment. With the main traps asleep, the rejects activate themselves out of sheer jealousy at being passed over for the final design. They are desperate, dysfunctional, and deeply committed to being taken seriously.

This is the campaign's last laugh. The final comedy episode before the endgame. The rejected traps are genuinely funny. The dungeon is genuinely entertaining. And buried in the fun, three things happen that change everything:

1. **[[EyeQ]] deploys to the field for the first time** — the Crown's architect needs to personally verify the Binder's paradox architectures. Alone with the PCs in a dungeon, far from [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]], [[EyeQ]] drops the last of his careful hedging
2. **[[Keraptis]] wakes up** — not with [[Acererak]]'s explosive rage but with the bone-deep exhaustion of a dungeon architect who has been killed, resurrected, robbed, and killed again so many times that dying is basically a vacation. He warns the PCs: his rejected designs were rejected for a REASON, and someone building with them is building on bad foundations
3. **The PCs encounter [[Grindle Gruk'tak]]** — a terrified goblin running a potion stall at the mountain's base, carrying a 49gp IOU from a beholder who borrowed money for a cape and a mixtape centuries ago. The PCs pocket it. They don't understand what they're holding. Not yet

**Tone arc:** Uneasy excitement (briefing — [[EyeQ]] deployed, Crown almost complete) → Quiet character moment ([[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] at the base camp) → Absurdist comedy (the rejected traps — each one funnier and more dysfunctional than the last) → Genuine unease (the Binder's contents and [[EyeQ]]'s revelation) → Weary confrontation ([[Keraptis]], exhausted and warning) → Controlled tension (escape through reactivating mountain) → The turn (debrief — ten down, two to go, and the IOU sits in someone's pocket like a bomb that hasn't gone off yet)

**Primary challenges:** Four comedy puzzle encounters (the rejected traps), exploration of a dormant legendary dungeon, a non-combat confrontation with [[Keraptis]], and escape through a reactivating trap gauntlet
**Combat:** Minimal — this is a puzzle/social/exploration adventure. One optional combat during escape. The real danger is the mountain waking up around the PCs

## Target Item
**[[The Keraptis Binder]]** — A thick leather tome, 14 inches tall, bound with brass clasps that have corroded green with age. The leather is cracked, water-stained, and covered in scorch marks from proximity to geothermal vents. It smells like sulfur and academic frustration.

The Binder contains every trap design [[Keraptis]] rejected during White Plume Mountain's construction — spatial paradoxes, temporal loops, perceptual distortion fields, and mechanical configurations that don't obey the laws of physics. Each page contains a detailed schematic, annotated in [[Keraptis]]'s meticulous hand, accompanied by notes explaining why the design was rejected: "Too unstable," "Spatial loop collapses after 47 iterations," "Test subjects went inside out (all of them)," "This one works but makes the user nauseous (unacceptable)."

The rejected designs all share one quality: they are **paradoxes**. Configurations that shouldn't exist. Spaces that are bigger on the inside than the outside. Corridors that are simultaneously 10 feet and 100 feet long. Doors that open into themselves. Traps that trigger before they're activated. Each one violates some fundamental law of spatial physics, and each one ALMOST works — close enough to function briefly before collapsing under its own contradiction.

**Properties:**
- **Spatial distortion (passive):** Reading the Binder for more than 10 minutes causes mild disorientation — the reader perceives nearby spaces as slightly wrong. Doorways look narrower than they are. Corridors appear to curve when they don't. Fort DC 14 after 10 minutes of reading or *sickened* for 1d4 rounds from perceptual mismatch. The effect fades within 1 hour of closing the Binder
- **Paradox architecture:** The Binder's schematics contain spatial configurations that function as self-referencing loops — systems that sustain themselves through internal contradiction. Like an Escher staircase that actually works. These configurations are useless as traps (they're too unstable) but invaluable as structural elements for something that needs to maintain itself without external support
- **[[Keraptis]]'s annotations:** The margins are dense with notes, corrections, and self-arguments. [[Keraptis]]'s design process is visible: he builds, tests, fails, analyzes the failure, and either fixes or discards. The discarded designs are marked with a single word: "SHELVED." Not destroyed. Not deleted. Shelved. He couldn't bring himself to throw them away. Even failed ideas have value — [[Keraptis]] just never found the right application

**[[The Device|Crown Infinite]] Purpose:** Paradox engineering — the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] needs to exist permanently, without external maintenance, without power sources, without anyone keeping it running. It needs to be SELF-SUSTAINING — a perpetual motion machine of metaphysical authority. [[Keraptis]]'s paradox architectures provide the template: systems that feed themselves through contradiction. A loop that generates its own energy by violating the law that says perpetual motion is impossible. The Crown doesn't need to obey physics. It needs to REPLACE physics. And to do that, it needs blueprints from someone who already tried to break spatial law — even if those blueprints were rejected. Especially if they were rejected. The Crown doesn't need designs that work within the rules. It needs designs that break them.

## Scene 1: The Briefing

### Setup
The back room. The circuits in the walls are no longer subtle — they pulse with visible golden light in complex patterns, branching and reconnecting like a nervous system coming online. The Workshop hum has become a chord: nine artifacts harmonizing, each one contributing a frequency. The Halo's divine light. The Pearl's ancient memory. The Egg's screaming grief. [[The Iris Prime]]'s steady ego-throb. The Key's ontological truth. Together they create something that sounds less like machinery and more like a heartbeat. [[Offledger]] has a pulse now. And it beats in time with [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]].

[[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] is different. He has been different for several quests, but the change is accelerating. The golden circuits on his hide are no longer tracework — they're **architecture**. Visible channels of light running across his body in the same patterns that cover the Workshop's walls. His central eye has a permanent golden tint. When he speaks, the circuits pulse in time with his words, as if the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] is listening to everything he says and agreeing.

He is becoming the device. Or the device is becoming him. The distinction stopped mattering three artifacts ago.

### [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s Pitch

> *"Last easy one."*

He says this without the usual showmanship. No pun. No grill flash. Just a statement.

> *"White Plume Mountain. You know it — everybody knows it. [[Keraptis]] built it. Big traps, big weapons, big reputation. But [[Keraptis]] was a perfectionist, and perfectionists throw things away. The mountain's got a whole wing of designs he rejected — trap schematics that didn't make the final cut. Paradox architectures. Impossible spaces. Stuff that breaks the rules of how space works."*

He pauses. The circuits on his hide pulse.

> *"I need those designs. The Crown needs structures that sustain themselves — loops that run forever without anybody maintaining them. [[Keraptis]] figured out how to build those loops. Then he decided they were too unstable and shelved them. What he doesn't know is that 'too unstable for a trap' is PERFECT for what I'm building."*

**If PCs ask why the rejected designs specifically:**

> *"Because the ones that WORK follow the rules. I don't need rules. I need the ones that BREAK rules. The paradoxes. The impossibilities. The designs that shouldn't exist. Those are the ones the Crown needs — systems that maintain themselves through contradiction. You can't run a reality-rewriting device on physics that physics APPROVES of."*

This is the most technically transparent [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] has ever been about the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]. He's not hiding it anymore. He's too close to completion to bother with pretense.

### [[EyeQ]]'s Deployment

> *"And I'm sending [[EyeQ]]."*

The room shifts. [[EyeQ]] floats forward from the Workshop stairway. The circuits on his shell are no longer pulsing — they're steady, constant, blazing gold. [[EyeQ]] looks less like a beholder-kin and more like a schematic that learned to float. The analytical mind behind the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]], deployed to the field for the first time.

> *"The Binder's gotta be verified on-site. The paradox architectures need to be stable enough to integrate — if they're too broken, they'll collapse the Crown instead of sustaining it. [[EyeQ]]'s the only one who can assess that in real-time."*

[[EyeQ]] speaks. His voice is the same — flat, precise, devoid of inflection:

> *"The paradox verification requires direct observation of spatial anomalies. Remote analysis is insufficient. I will accompany the retrieval team."*

He means the PCs. He called them the "retrieval team." Not "the adventurers." Not "the employees." The retrieval team. They are components in a process, and [[EyeQ]] has always seen them that way.

**If PCs note that [[EyeQ]] has never been in the field:**

[[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] glances at [[EyeQ]]. Something passes between them — not spoken, not telepathic. Just the understanding of two beings who have worked on the same project for centuries.

> *"[[EyeQ]] goes where the work needs him. The work needs him in the mountain."*

### Logistics

- **Portal:** A door in [[Offledger]]'s north wall — previously a supply closet — opens onto a hillside trail 2 miles from White Plume Mountain. Two-way portal, 24-hour window
- **Dormant cycle:** The mountain enters a dormant phase every 11 years when [[Keraptis]] sleeps. During dormancy, the main traps are powered down. The legendary weapons (Blackrazor, Wave, Whelm) are inert in their display chambers. The dungeon is theoretically safe. *"Theoretically. [[Keraptis]] built this thing. 'Safe' is relative"*
- **Timeline:** Dormancy lasts 72 hours. The PCs have arrived on hour 8. The mountain wakes on hour 72 regardless of whether [[Keraptis]] does. The PCs need to be out before the main traps reactivate
- **Equipment:** Standard kit. No special provisions. *"This one's a library run, not a war. Go in, grab the book, come home."*
- **[[EyeQ]]'s role:** Field verification. He will assess the Binder's contents on-site and confirm compatibility with the Crown's architecture. He is NOT combat support. He is an analyst. If the PCs need someone killed, they'll have to do it themselves

### DM Notes
- **[[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s lack of showmanship is the scene's most important detail.** "Last easy one" — no pun, no performance. He's exhausted in a way the PCs haven't seen before. Not physically. Psychically. Nine artifacts are orbiting in the Workshop. The Crown's heartbeat fills [[Offledger]]. The circuits are crawling across [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s body. He's so close to completion that the anticipation is consuming him. The comedy is running out. After this, there's only the work
- **[[EyeQ]]'s deployment changes the party dynamic entirely.** For the first time, the PCs have the Crown's architect as their companion — alone, in a dungeon, far from [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s oversight. Every previous [[EyeQ]] interaction has been observed, witnessed, deniable. Inside White Plume Mountain, [[EyeQ]] has the freedom to say things he's never said before. The PCs should feel the opportunity. And the danger
- **"I need the ones that BREAK rules" is [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s design philosophy stated plainly.** The [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] doesn't work within reality — it replaces reality. It needs components that violate the laws of physics, metaphysics, and causality. This is why the artifacts are so eclectic: a truth-enforcing key, a screaming egg, crystallized ego, a demilich's failed experiments. The Crown is built from things that shouldn't exist. [[Keraptis]]'s paradoxes are the structural blueprint for impossibility itself

## Scene 2: The Base Camp

### The Mountain
White Plume Mountain is unmistakable — a volcanic peak rising from swampy lowlands, venting a constant plume of white steam from its geothermal core. The steam is visible for miles, a perpetual banner announcing: something powerful lives here. Even dormant, the mountain radiates presence. The stone is warm to the touch. The air tastes like sulfur and old magic. The ground vibrates faintly — not the Crown's pulse, but the mountain's own heartbeat. It's sleeping, not dead.

### Stonekettle
At the mountain's base, where the geothermal heat meets the swamp, a small settlement has grown around the hot springs: **Stonekettle**. Thirty-odd residents — mostly humans and halflings — who make their living selling supplies to adventurers foolish enough to challenge the mountain. A tavern (The Last Drink), a general store, a shrine to several gods of protection (ecumenical, because adventurers pray to anything before entering White Plume Mountain), and a half-dozen vendor stalls clustered around the main hot spring.

The locals are friendly in the way of people who've learned that their customers usually die: warmly, and with efficient transactions.

### [[Grindle Gruk'tak]]
At the far end of the vendor row, almost hidden behind a larger stall, sits a shabby potion booth. The sign reads "GRINDLE'S MOSTLY GOOD POTIONS" in handwritten Common, with a smaller note: "*Also Poisons (Legal Ones Only) (Mostly)*"

**[[Grindle Gruk'tak]]** is a goblin. Middle-aged, which for goblins means he's survived longer than anyone expected, including himself. He's small — even for a goblin — with oversized ears, a permanent squint, and hands stained with alchemical residue. His inventory is modest: healing potions (slightly discounted, slightly weak — CL1 instead of CL3), antitoxins, alchemist's fire, and a  "*mystery potion*" that he guarantees does *something*.

He's nervous. He's always nervous. Goblins who live among larger races develop a baseline anxiety that never fully dissipates, and [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] has been anxious for forty-three years.

**When the PCs approach:** He's eager for a sale. Adventurers heading to the mountain always buy potions. His prices are fair. His patter is charming in a desperate way:

> *"Potions! Good potions! Healing, mostly. Some of them taste okay. This one—"* (holding up a murky vial) *"—I'm not sure what it does but it's been green for three months so it's probably stable."*

**The IOU Trigger:** One of two things causes [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] to notice something:

**Option A — The PCs are wearing or carrying anything with [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s sigil** (the eye-and-coins emblem from their convention uniforms, or any item branded by [[The Coinspiratorium (Location)|the Coinspiratorium]]). [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] sees it. He freezes. His ears flatten against his skull.

> *"That... that symbol. The eye with the coins."*

He swallows hard.

> *"You work for a beholder. Big one. Chains. Talks a lot. Calls himself something fancy."*

**Option B — [[EyeQ]] is visible.** A floating beholder-kin browsing the hot springs marketplace is not subtle. [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] sees [[EyeQ]] and goes pale green (paler than his usual green).

> *"That's... that's one of his. One of the floating ones. Oh no. Oh no no no."*

### The IOU

Either way, [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]]'s nervousness spikes. He looks around, checking if anyone is watching. Then, with the frantic energy of someone who's been carrying a weight for decades and suddenly sees a chance to put it down:

> *"Listen. Listen. I don't want trouble. I've never wanted trouble. I just want to sell potions and not get disintegrated. But your boss — the beholder — he owes me money."*

He digs under his booth's counter. Rummages through a box of receipts, dried herbs, and what appears to be a pet beetle. Pulls out a piece of folded parchment, yellowed with age, creased from being handled a thousand times.

> *"Forty-nine gold. He borrowed it from me. A long time ago — before he was... what he is now. Before the chains and the floating and the crime empire. He was just a beholder. A small one. Scared. Needed money for—"*

[[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] looks embarrassed on [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s behalf.

> *"—a cape. And some copies of a mixtape. I'm not making this up. It's all on the paper."*

He holds out the IOU.

It's a simple document. Handwritten in shaky Draconic on cheap parchment. It reads:

> *BORROWER: [[Vizzok the Opulent]]*
> *LENDER: [[Grindle Gruk'tak]]*
> *AMOUNT: 49 gold pieces*
> *PURPOSE: 1 Vision Cape (37gp), 10 copies "Eyes on the Horizon" demo (12gp)*
> *TERMS: Repayment upon borrower's first profitable venture*
> *SIGNED: [a tentacle print] [a goblin thumbprint]*
> *DATE: [over 400 years ago]*

It's real. The tentacle print matches [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s (any PC who has seen [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s signature on contracts can confirm with a DC 12 check). The debt has never been repaid. The "first profitable venture" condition was met centuries ago. The IOU is, by any legal definition, overdue.

**[[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]]'s plea:**

> *"I'm not asking for trouble. I KNOW I can't collect from him. He'd disintegrate me. He'd disintegrate my STALL. He'd disintegrate the mountain and everything on it and probably enjoy doing it. I just... I've been carrying this for so long. And you work for him. So maybe... maybe you could mention it? Or just... take it. Get it away from me. I don't want it anymore. I never wanted it. I just wanted my forty-nine gold."*

He pushes the IOU into the nearest PC's hands.

> *"Please. Take it. I don't care about the money anymore. I just want to stop being scared that one day he'll remember I exist and come looking."*

**If PCs ask what [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] was like back then:**

> *"Small. Quiet. Only a few eyestalks working right. He came through here once — I think he was running from something. Needed money fast. I had savings. Forty-nine gold — that was everything I had. He said he'd pay me back. He seemed honest. Then he left. And then, years later, I started hearing stories about a beholder crime lord called '[[Vizzok the Opulent|Lord Veezy]]' and I thought: oh. Oh no."*

[[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]]'s eyes are wide. Not with anger — with the residual awe of someone who lent money to a nobody who became a god.

> *"He was gonna be something. Even then. You could tell. But forty-nine gold is forty-nine gold, and a deal's a deal."*

### [[EyeQ]]'s Reaction
[[EyeQ]] observes the exchange. If the PCs show him the IOU, his eyestalks orient on the document with a focus that borders on physical intensity.

He says nothing. His circuits — the steady golden glow — flicker. Just once. The tiniest disruption in the constant light. Then the glow stabilizes.

> *"Noted."*

That's all. One word. But the flicker said everything. [[EyeQ]], the architect, the being who understands the Crown's mathematics better than anyone, just saw a 49gp IOU and his circuits disrupted.

The PCs may not understand why. Not yet.

### DM Notes
- **THE GRINDLE ENCOUNTER IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SCENE IN THE ENTIRE CAMPAIGN.** Not the loudest. Not the most dramatic. Not the most emotional. But the most important. The PCs just received the weapon that will destroy the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]], and they have no idea. The IOU looks like nothing — a goblin's complaint about a centuries-old debt. A 49gp loan for a cape and a mixtape. The PCs will likely pocket it, maybe laugh about it, maybe promise [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] they'll mention it to [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]. They will not understand what they're holding until [[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]] explains the math
- **Play [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] as completely, utterly mundane.** He's not a quest-giver. He's not a sage. He's not a hidden ally. He's a scared goblin who sells discount potions and wants to stop being afraid. The ordinariness is the weapon — the IOU's power comes from its insignificance. Under the Crown's own Law II (Debt Becomes Destiny), a creditor cannot owe. If the Crown's master creditor has an outstanding debt, the system cannot reconcile the contradiction. The IOU is a divide-by-zero error in the fabric of reality. And it looks like a receipt for a cape
- **[[EyeQ]]'s flicker is the only hint.** [[EyeQ]] recognized the IOU's significance INSTANTLY. He didn't need [[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]]'s math — he IS the math. He designed the Crown. He knows that a pre-existing debt owed by the master creditor is a catastrophic vulnerability. And he said "Noted." One word. The most dangerous word he's ever spoken. Because "noted" means "I have registered this information and I am choosing not to tell Lord Vizzok." [[EyeQ]] just committed his first act of potential betrayal, and he did it with a single syllable
- **[[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]] should feel REAL.** Not a plot device. A person. A goblin who lent money to someone who turned out to be a monster, and who has spent decades being too scared to ask for it back. The PCs should feel for him. When the Crown falls — if the IOU is the weapon that does it — the PCs should remember [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]]'s face. The most important person in the campaign is a potion seller who just wanted his forty-nine gold

## Scene 3: The Rejected Wing

### Entering the Mountain
White Plume Mountain's main entrance is sealed during dormancy — the great stone doors are shut, the exterior traps are inert, and the steam vents have reduced to a steady hiss. The PCs enter through a maintenance tunnel on the eastern slope: a low, humid corridor carved through volcanic rock, lit by the dull red glow of geothermal veins in the stone. The air is warm and mineral-thick. The walls are slick with condensation.

The maintenance tunnel leads past the sealed main dungeon — the PCs can see the dormant trap corridors through iron gratings. Everything is still. Dust covers the mechanisms. Blackrazor, somewhere in the darkness, sleeps.

[[EyeQ]] floats behind the party, eyestalks scanning every surface. He's cataloguing. Assessing. His circuits cast golden light on the wet stone. He does not speak unless spoken to. He is doing *work*.

The tunnel terminates at a heavy iron door marked with [[Keraptis]]'s personal sigil (a stylized mountain inside a circle) and the words, in Common: **"ARCHIVE — PROTOTYPE STORAGE — DO NOT ENTER DURING ACTIVE CYCLE."**

The door is unlocked. Dormant cycle.

Beyond it: the Rejected Wing.

### The Wing
The Rejected Wing is a series of chambers and corridors branching off from a central hallway. Unlike the main dungeon — which is immaculate, precisely engineered, every trap a masterwork — the Rejected Wing is **messy**. Construction materials are stacked in corners. Diagrams are pinned to walls with iron nails. Test dummies (wooden, scorched, some melted) line the corridors like failed crash test subjects. The air smells like old experiments and regret.

This is where [[Keraptis]] built, tested, and discarded designs. It's a workshop, a laboratory, and a graveyard for ideas that didn't work.

**The rejected traps have activated themselves.** The main traps are dormant. But the rejects — powered by their own self-sustaining paradox loops — don't follow the dormancy cycle. They're independent. They've been waiting. And when the PCs entered the Wing, the rejects woke up with the frantic energy of understudies told the lead has called in sick.

### Trap 1: The Room Full of Aggressively Neutral Bureaucrats

**The Chamber:** A 30-foot square room that has been transformed into what can only be described as a government office. Stone desks arranged in rows. Illusory constructs sitting behind each desk — humanoid figures in grey robes, each one featureless except for a mouth that speaks and hands that stamp documents. There are twelve of them. They are THRILLED to have visitors.

> ***"WELCOME TO THE WHITE PLUME MOUNTAIN REGULATORY COMPLIANCE OFFICE. PLEASE TAKE A NUMBER AND PROCEED TO THE NEAREST AVAILABLE CLERK."***

A small stone tablet near the entrance dispenses number tokens. The PCs' number is 4,847.

> ***"NOW SERVING: NUMBER 4,846."***

Pause.

> ***"NUMBER 4,846 HAS NOT RESPONDED. NOW SERVING: NUMBER 4,847."***

**The mechanic:** The bureaucrats will not let the PCs pass through the room's exit until they have completed Form 7-B: "Declaration of Intent to Traverse a Paradox-Enabled Space." The form requires:
- Full legal names (all of them, including middle names, titles, and any names acquired through contract — yes, [[The Coinspiratorium (Location)|the Coinspiratorium]] employment contract counts)
- Intended duration of stay (in business days)
- Reason for visit (select from: Tourism, Academic Research, Theft, Other — the form includes "Theft" as a standard option)
- Emergency contact information "in the event of spatial paradox-related dissolution"
- A signature acknowledging that "the White Plume Mountain Regulatory Compliance Office assumes no liability for injuries sustained through interaction with rejected prototypes"

**The problem:** Completing the form takes 20 minutes. The PCs don't have 20 minutes (the dormancy window is limited). Also, one of the fields requires "Authorization Code from the Mountain's Primary Architect," which is [[Keraptis]], who is asleep.

**Solutions:**
- **Fill in the forms with deliberate contradictions.** Name: "Yes." Duration: "Negative three days." Reason: "All of the above simultaneously." The bureaucrats cannot process contradictory information. Their illusion matrices overload and they freeze, flickering, for 2 rounds — enough to walk through
- **Diplomacy DC 18:** Convince the lead bureaucrat that the PCs have "pre-authorized emergency access" — technically true if interpreted loosely
- **Destroy the desks.** The bureaucrats are tied to their desks. No desk, no bureaucrat. The desks have 15 hp each and hardness 5. But destroying them triggers a secondary response: a booming voice ([[Keraptis]]'s, recorded) saying *"PLEASE DO NOT VANDALIZE THE COMPLIANCE OFFICE. THIS IS THE ONLY THING IN THIS WING I'M NOT EMBARRASSED ABOUT"*
- **Just walk through.** The bureaucrats cannot physically prevent passage. They can only loudly, persistently demand compliance. A PC who walks through without filing paperwork is marked as "NON-COMPLIANT" and followed by a floating illusory stamp that hovers above their head reading "REGULATORY VIOLATION" for 1d4 hours. It has no mechanical effect. It is deeply annoying

**[[EyeQ]]'s reaction:** He files the form. Correctly. In 45 seconds. Every field completed with precise, technically accurate information. The bureaucrats process it, stamp it, and wave him through. He doesn't wait for the PCs.

> *"The form was not complicated."*

### Trap 2: The Wet Floor Which Continues Forever

**The Corridor:** A 60-foot stone hallway. The floor is wet. Not dangerously wet — just perpetually, inexplicably damp. There's a sign at the entrance: "CAUTION: WET FLOOR." The sign has been here for centuries. The floor has been wet for centuries. The sign is correct.

**The mechanic:** The corridor loops. Walking forward, the PCs reach the far end after 60 feet — and find themselves at the beginning again. The same wet floor. The same sign. The same 60 feet. They're walking the same stretch of corridor in an infinite loop.

The loop is a spatial paradox — a section of space that connects its end to its beginning. It only works in one direction (forward). [[Keraptis]]'s notes on the wall (discoverable with Search DC 14):

> *"Spatial recursion prototype. Loop is stable in forward direction only. Backward traversal breaks the recursion and exits to true corridor. SHELVED — impractical as trap (victims simply walk backward). Potential structural application? TODO: revisit."*

**Solution:** Walk backward. The PCs must physically reverse direction and walk backward through the corridor. This breaks the loop and deposits them in the actual continuation of the Wing.

**Complications:**
- Walking backward through a wet corridor: Balance DC 12 or slip and fall (1d3 damage, more embarrassment)
- A PC who RUNS backward (trying to speed through): Balance DC 18
- If the PCs don't find the note: trial and error eventually reveals the backward solution, but they lose 10-15 minutes testing approaches (reducing their dormancy window)

**[[EyeQ]]'s reaction:** He reads the note. He looks at the corridor. He looks at the note again.

> *"A self-referencing spatial loop using recursive geometry. The forward vector creates closure while the reverse vector exists outside the loop's reference frame. This is—"*

He pauses. His circuits pulse.

> *"This is elegant. The recursion is stable. Indefinitely stable. [[Keraptis]] shelved this because it fails as a trap, but the underlying architecture is..."*

He stops himself. Looks at the PCs.

> *"...relevant to my work."*

This is [[EyeQ]] seeing the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]'s structural principle in the wild. The loop sustains itself through paradox. The Crown does the same thing. [[EyeQ]] recognizing the connection should be unsettling.

### Trap 3: Too Many Keys, None Are Correct

**The Door:** A massive iron door, 10 feet tall, covered in keyholes. Forty-seven keyholes of varying sizes, arranged in concentric circles around a central boss. Each keyhole is ornately crafted. Each one looks important. Each one looks like THE keyhole.

On a shelf beside the door: forty-seven keys, each uniquely shaped, each labeled with a number.

**The mechanic:** None of the keys open the door. The door is a **push door**. It opens by pushing. There is no lock. The keyholes are decorative anxiety — designed to make intruders waste time trying every key while the correct solution is the simplest possible action.

**Complications:**
- **The keyholes are trapped.** Seven of the forty-seven keyholes are rigged: inserting a key triggers a response. Three deliver an electric shock (1d6 lightning, Reflex DC 14 to drop the key in time). Two release a spray of ink that blinds the PC for 1 round. One emits a loud buzzer sound. One fires a spring-loaded boxing glove that deals 1d4 bludgeoning (non-lethal) — [[Keraptis]]'s margin note on this one reads: *"FUNNY BUT NOT FATAL. RECONSIDER?"*
- **The keys are heavy.** Each key weighs 3 pounds. Carrying all forty-seven is 141 pounds. The shelf is designed so that removing keys feels like progress, encouraging the PCs to collect more and more keys while missing the obvious solution
- **A DC 18 Search check on the door** reveals: no lock mechanism behind ANY keyhole. The keyholes are solid metal behind the first inch — decorative only
- **A DC 14 Intelligence check (or player insight):** "Wait. What if we just push it?"

**[[EyeQ]]'s reaction:** He pushes the door open without comment. He read the spatial engineering and recognized immediately that a paradox architect wouldn't waste a functional lock on a door. He says nothing. His silence is the comment.

### Trap 4: The Accidental Nude Teleportation Ring

**The Chamber:** A circular room, 20 feet in diameter, with a raised platform in the center. On the platform: a teleportation circle, glowing faintly blue, inscribed with standard conjuration runes. It's clearly a teleporter. A sign next to it reads:

> *"PROTOTYPE PERSONNEL TRANSPORTER — CHAMBER 4 TO ARCHIVE*
> *STATUS: FUNCTIONAL*
> *NOTE: TRANSPORTS ORGANIC MATTER ONLY."*

Below that, in different ink, hastier handwriting:

> *"CORRECTION: Transports organic matter EXCLUSIVELY. Inorganic matter (clothing, equipment, armor, weapons, EVERYTHING ELSE) does not transport. All items remain at origin point. Personnel arrive at destination... unencumbered."*

Below THAT, in red ink:

> *"DO NOT DEMONSTRATE FOR VISITORS. AGAIN."*

**The mechanic:** The teleporter works perfectly. It transports any living creature standing on the platform directly to the Archive (Scene 4's destination). However, it ONLY transports organic matter. Clothing, armor, weapons, spell components, magic items, bags, boots, hats, cloaks, rings, and anything else that isn't part of the PC's body stays behind on the platform in a neat pile.

The PC arrives at the destination naked. Their gear arrives 1d4 rounds later via a secondary transport pulse ([[Keraptis]] added this as a patch after the first "incident"), scattered around the arrival room.

**Solutions:**
- **Use it.** Arrive naked. Retrieve gear. Continue. Fastest route to the Archive. The comedy writes itself
- **Don't use it.** Take the long way around — a corridor that bypasses the teleporter adds 20 minutes to the journey
- **Modify the circle:** A DC 22 Spellcraft check allows a PC to temporarily adjust the teleporter's parameters to include inorganic matter. This takes 15 minutes and requires a DC 20 Knowledge (arcana) check to avoid the "patch" overloading and teleporting the PC's gear to a random location in the mountain
- **Strip first.** A PC who removes all equipment BEFORE stepping on the circle arrives clothed in nothing (as expected) but their gear can be carried through the long corridor by another party member and reunited

**[[EyeQ]] uses the teleporter without hesitation.** He has no clothing. No equipment. No possessions. He IS what he carries. He arrives at the destination instantly, circuits blazing, completely unbothered.

> *"The transporter functions within expected parameters."*

The PCs, if they followed, are scrambling for their scattered gear. [[EyeQ]] does not acknowledge the comedy.

### DM Notes
- **Each rejected trap should be played for maximum comedy, then followed by a beat of genuine insight.** The bureaucrats are funny — but [[EyeQ]] filing the form perfectly reveals his inhuman precision. The wet floor is funny — but [[EyeQ]] recognizing the recursion as "relevant to my work" is chilling. The keys are funny — but [[EyeQ]]'s silent push reveals he's been assessing everything. The teleporter is funny — but [[EyeQ]] walking through unaffected is a reminder that he's not human, not mortal, not like them
- **The rejected traps have PERSONALITY.** They're not just mechanisms — they're ideas that wanted to be important. The bureaucrats are desperate for visitors. The wet floor loops forever because it has nothing else to do. The keyholes want to be tried so badly they'll shock you for the attention. The teleporter keeps functioning because [[Keraptis]] patched it instead of dismantling it. These traps were loved once. Then shelved. The parallel to [[The Eyecons|the Eyecons]] — beloved creations destined to be consumed by something bigger — should resonate
- **[[EyeQ]]'s behavior across all four traps is a character study.** He files forms. He recognizes architecture. He ignores puzzles. He teleports without flinching. He is the most efficient, most alien, most competent companion the PCs have ever had. He is also the most dangerous, because every observation he makes is data for the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]. The PCs are walking through a dungeon with the device's architect, and every paradox they encounter is a blueprint he's memorizing

## Scene 4: The Binder

### The Archive
Beyond the rejected traps, the Wing terminates in a vault carved from the mountain's volcanic core. The walls are warm — not dangerously hot, but warm the way living skin is warm. The mountain breathes here. Small vents release sulfuric steam in rhythmic intervals. The room smells like the earth's interior.

Stone shelves line the walls, filled with schematics, models, and prototypes:
- Scale models of trap configurations, some melted, some frozen, one apparently turned inside out
- Stacks of drafting paper covered in geometric calculations and increasingly frustrated annotations
- A shelf labeled "WORKING" (three items) and a shelf labeled "NOT WORKING (YET)" (overflowing)
- A framed commendation from the Archmage's Guild for "Excellence in Spatial Engineering" — hung crookedly, with a note taped to it: *"The ONLY recognition I've ever received and it's from THAT guild. Insulting."*

At the center of the room, on a stone lectern surrounded by protective runes (dormant): **the [[Keraptis]] Binder**.

### The Binder
It's heavier than the PCs expected. The brass clasps resist opening — not locked, just stiff from centuries of disuse. When the cover opens, the pages release a smell like hot metal and broken geometry.

The contents are dense. Each page contains a trap schematic rendered in precise draftsmanship — [[Keraptis]]'s engineering notation, spatial coordinates, magical field equations, and annotations. The designs are strange. Not just complex — WRONG. The spatial relationships depicted on each page violate Euclidean geometry. Lines that should be parallel converge. Spaces that should be contained overflow their boundaries. Corridors that are drawn as 10 feet long are labeled as 100 feet long AND 10 feet long simultaneously.

Reading more than a page at a time causes the disorientation effect (Fort DC 14 or *sickened* 1d4 rounds). The brain rebels against the paradoxes.

**Key annotations found in the Binder:**

Page 12: *"Self-referencing spatial loop — stable recursion through geometric contradiction. Sustains itself indefinitely without external power. SHELVED: too stable. Victims adapt. Not lethal enough."*

Page 47: *"Temporal stutter trap — causes the target to experience the same 6 seconds repeatedly. SHELVED: target retains memory across iterations. They learn the trap pattern and escape. Paradox collapses after ~200 iterations."*

Page 89: *"Dimensional overlap zone — two spaces occupying the same location simultaneously. SHELVED: test subjects experience both spaces at once and develop severe dissociative disorders. Also: impossible to clean."*

Page 134: *"Self-sustaining paradox matrix — a system of spatial contradictions that maintain each other through mutual reference. Each contradiction stabilizes the others. The whole structure is impossible but persists because each individual impossibility is supported by the rest. SHELVED: I have no idea what to USE this for. It's the most beautiful thing I've ever designed and it has no purpose. Maybe someone else will find one."*

Page 134 is the one [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] wants. The self-sustaining paradox matrix. A system of contradictions that maintain each other — exactly what the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] needs to run forever without external support.

### [[EyeQ]] at the Binder
[[EyeQ]] reads page 134. His eyestalks extend to maximum length. Every analytical appendage focuses on the schematic with an intensity the PCs have never seen from any Eyecon.

Silence. Thirty seconds. A full minute.

> *"This is it."*

He says it quietly. Not to the PCs. To the page.

> *"This is the structural basis for perpetual coherence. Mutual contradiction as load-bearing architecture. Each paradox supports the others. The system is impossible, and therefore indestructible, because destroying any single contradiction is prevented by the others."*

He looks up at the PCs. His circuits are blazing.

> *"The [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]'s defensive architecture has been missing this principle. With this framework, the device becomes... permanent. Self-sustaining. It doesn't need power. It doesn't need maintenance. It doesn't need [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]."*

He catches himself. The circuits flicker.

> *"It needs [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] to ACTIVATE. But once activated... the paradox matrix sustains it independently. Lord Vizzok becomes the ignition key. Not the engine."*

### [[EyeQ]]'s Revelation
This is the moment. Alone in a volcanic archive, surrounded by an exhausted wizard's abandoned designs, [[EyeQ]] tells the PCs the truth. Not all of it. But more than he's ever told anyone.

> *"I designed the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]. Every circuit. Every connection. Every law it will enforce. I know how it works better than Lord Vizzok does, because he is the vision and I am the architecture."*

He hovers lower. Closer to the PCs. His voice drops — not for secrecy (there's no one to overhear) but from weight.

> *"I also know how it fails."*

Silence.

> *"The Crown is built on a paradox: a universal creditor who owes nothing. Law II states that debt becomes destiny. The system requires a master creditor — a being at the top of all obligation, who is owed by everything and owes nothing. Lord Vizzok."*

He pauses.

> *"If Lord Vizzok owes a debt — any debt, however small, however old — the system cannot resolve. A creditor who is also a debtor creates recursive obligation. The interest compounds infinitely. The ledger cannot balance. The Crown... fractures."*

He looks at the PCs. Specifically, he looks at whichever PC is carrying [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]]'s IOU.

> *"I assume you understand why I mention this."*

### DM Notes
- **[[EyeQ]]'s revelation is the campaign's most dangerous conversation.** The Crown's architect just told the PCs how to destroy it. Not through hints, not through careful hedging — directly. "If Lord Vizzok owes a debt, the system cannot resolve." Combined with the IOU in the PC's pocket, this is the complete endgame weapon. The PCs now have the instrument (the IOU) and the instructions ([[EyeQ]]'s explanation). Everything from here is choice
- **"I also know how it fails" is [[EyeQ]]'s act of betrayal.** He has served [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] for centuries. He designed the Crown. He knows that activation will consume him — dissolve his consciousness into the device's architecture, making him a permanent, unthinking structural component. And he has decided, quietly, analytically, without emotion, that he does not consent. [[EyeQ]] is not rebelling. [[EyeQ]] is providing information. What the PCs do with it is their responsibility. That's always been [[EyeQ]]'s nature: he doesn't act. He informs
- **Page 134 — "the most beautiful thing I've ever designed and it has no purpose" — is [[Keraptis]]'s gift to the Crown.** He created something impossible and couldn't find a use for it. [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] found the use. The [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] runs on other people's failures: [[Acererak]]'s rejected persistence framework, [[Keraptis]]'s shelved paradox matrix, a sterile dragon egg, a key that can only be used once. Every component is something that was supposed to be something else and wasn't. The Crown is built from the multiverse's broken promises. And that — as [[Rue Kethani]] would say — sounds familiar

## Scene 5: [[Keraptis]]

### The Awakening
Twelve minutes after the Binder is removed from its lectern, the mountain shudders. Not the geothermal tremor of a sleeping volcano — a deliberate vibration, rhythmic, purposeful. Like something turning over in bed. Like something waking up.

[[Keraptis]] is awake.

### The Encounter
He doesn't arrive dramatically. No explosion of power. No booming voice. A door opens in the Archive's far wall — a door that wasn't there before — and a man walks through it.

**[[Keraptis]]** looks like an old man who has been old for a very long time and has stopped caring about it. Thin, stooped, wearing robes that were expensive four hundred years ago and have been repaired so many times that more patch than original remains. His hair is white, wild, and slightly singed. His eyes are sharp — sharply tired. He carries a staff that's clearly decorative (the crystal on top fell off decades ago and was replaced with a doorknob).

He looks at the PCs. He looks at the empty lectern. He looks at [[EyeQ]]. He looks at the Binder in the PCs' hands.

He sighs. It's the longest, most exhausted sigh the PCs have ever heard. It contains centuries of resigned annoyance.

> *"Again."*

He shuffles to a stone bench near the wall and sits down.

> *"Let me guess. Adventurers. Hired by someone. Here for my things. During the dormant cycle because you're smart enough to avoid the active traps and not smart enough to avoid the rejected ones."*

He rubs his eyes.

> *"This happens every cycle. I've been killed — actually killed, properly dead — fourteen times. Resurrected twelve. The other two times I came back on my own because apparently the afterlife doesn't want me either."*

### [[Keraptis]]'s Demeanor
He's not angry. He's not threatening. He's not [[Acererak]]. He's **tired**. The kind of tired that comes from being a recurring villain in a multiverse that won't let you stay dead.

> *"What's the target this time? Blackrazor? Everyone wants Blackrazor. Or is it Wave? Nobody ever wants Whelm. Poor Whelm."*

**When the PCs show the Binder:**

His expression changes. Not to anger — to something more complicated. Shame? Recognition? The weary acceptance of a creator confronted with work he abandoned?

> *"The Binder. My... rejects."*

He looks at it the way [[Acererak]] looked at his notebook. The way [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] looked at the egg. The way every creator looks at work they threw away.

> *"Those designs are shelved for a REASON. The paradox architectures are unstable. The spatial loops collapse under load. The self-referencing matrices are theoretically elegant and practically suicidal. I shelved them because BUILDING with them is building on foundations made of contradictions."*

He leans forward. For the first time, actual sharpness enters his voice.

> *"Who sent you?"*

### The Warning
If the PCs tell [[Keraptis]] about [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] (or enough for him to deduce it):

> *"A beholder. Building something. Using my rejected paradox work as structural architecture."*

He's quiet for a moment. Processing. Then:

> *"Do you know why I shelved the self-sustaining paradox matrix? It's not because it doesn't work. It works. It works PERFECTLY. It maintains itself through mutual contradiction — each impossibility supporting the others. Indestructible. Permanent. Beautiful."*

He stands. The tiredness doesn't leave, but something harder shows through it.

> *"The problem is what happens when one contradiction is RESOLVED. If a single paradox in the matrix is answered — if something that is supposed to be impossible becomes possible — the entire structure cascades. Every supporting contradiction fails. Every paradox collapses. The matrix doesn't degrade gradually. It shatters. Instantly. Completely."*

He looks at the PCs with the evaluating gaze of an engineer who knows exactly what they're building.

> *"If your employer is building a system sustained by mutual paradox, and that system has a single resolvable contradiction at its foundation..."*

He lets the sentence hang.

> *"...then whoever finds that contradiction holds the kill switch for his entire operation."*

**If PCs ask what kind of contradiction could bring down such a system:**

> *"Something simple. Something that breaks the foundational assumption. If the system assumes its architect is X, and the architect is actually Y — if there's a truth that contradicts the system's core identity — the paradox that holds everything together isn't a paradox anymore. It's just a mistake. And mistakes don't hold weight."*

He looks at the Binder in their hands.

> *"Take it. Tell your employer I said his architecture is built on shelved designs, and shelved designs were shelved for reasons he clearly hasn't bothered to understand."*

He shuffles back toward the door.

> *"And tell him to stop sending people during my dormant cycle. I'm trying to sleep."*

### DM Notes
- **[[Keraptis]] is the anti-[[Acererak]].** Where [[Acererak]] exploded with narcissistic rage, [[Keraptis]] responds with the exhaustion of someone who has been through this exact scenario too many times. He's been killed fourteen times. He's been robbed constantly. He's the D&D equivalent of a homeowner who's stopped locking his doors because the burglars just break the windows. Play him as genuinely tired, not comedically tired — a person who once cared deeply about his work and has had that care eroded by centuries of repetition
- **[[Keraptis]]'s warning is the campaign's technical briefing for the endgame.** He's explaining, in engineering terms, exactly why the IOU works: the Crown is a paradox matrix sustained by mutual contradiction. The foundational assumption is that the master creditor ([[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]) owes nothing. The IOU proves he owes 49gp. That's not a paradox — it's a resolvable contradiction. And resolved contradictions shatter paradox matrices. [[Keraptis]] just gave the PCs the theoretical framework for understanding what [[EyeQ]] told them in the Archive. Two independent sources. Same conclusion
- **"Shelved designs were shelved for reasons" is the adventure's thesis.** The [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] is built from other people's rejects — [[Acererak]]'s failed persistence models, [[Keraptis]]'s unstable paradoxes, a sterile egg, a used-up key. Every component was abandoned by its creator. [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] collected them because he saw applications their creators didn't. But the creators may have been right. The Crown is a monument built on other people's mistakes. Beautiful, ambitious, and fundamentally unstable. Just like [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] himself

## Scene 6: The Escape

### The Mountain Wakes
[[Keraptis]] woke up. Which means the mountain is waking up too — not on the 72-hour dormancy schedule, but NOW, triggered by its architect's consciousness returning.

The transition isn't instant. It's a gradient. As the PCs retrace their path through the Rejected Wing, they feel the mountain powering on:

**The Rejected Wing:** The rejected traps, already active, intensify. The bureaucrats in Trap 1 become AGGRESSIVE about compliance — they physically block passage, requiring the PCs to push through (Strength DC 14) while being pelted with regulation citations. The wet floor in Trap 2 becomes ACTUALLY slippery (Balance DC 16 or fall). The keyholes in Trap 3 all fire simultaneously — a barrage of shocks, ink, and boxing gloves. The teleporter in Trap 4 activates on proximity instead of by choice — anyone within 5 feet must make a Reflex save (DC 16) or be teleported naked to a random room in the wing.

**The Main Dungeon:** Through the iron gratings, the PCs can see the main dungeon activating. Trap mechanisms engaging. Lights igniting. The distant sound of metal grinding against stone. Somewhere deep in the mountain, Blackrazor hums — a low, hungry vibration that makes the PCs' weapons feel inadequate.

**Timeline:** The PCs have approximately 10 minutes to reach the maintenance tunnel exit before the main dungeon's security systems extend to the Wing. After that, they're facing White Plume Mountain at full power — a dungeon designed to kill experienced adventurers, now fully awake and irritated.

### Encounter: The Rejected Guardian
Between the Binder's archive and the first trap room, the mountain's reactivation shakes something loose. A section of wall crumbles, revealing an alcove that wasn't on any map — a bricked-up chamber containing [[Keraptis]]'s most embarrassing failure: an experimental guardian construct, assembled from mismatched parts and sealed away when it proved "temperamentally unsuitable."

It's awake now. And it's angry about being bricked up for three centuries.

**The Rejected Guardian** — CR 8 (modified Flesh Golem, MM p.135)
- Large Construct | HP 79 | AC 18 (-1 size, +9 natural) | Speed 30 ft. (halting, uneven)
- **2 Slams +10 melee** (2d8+5)
- **Berserk:** If the guardian takes damage in a single round exceeding 20 HP, it goes berserk — attacks the nearest creature regardless of allegiance, gains +2 attack/damage, -2 AC. Lasts until destroyed or no creatures remain in sight
- **Magic Immunity:** Immune to any spell or spell-like ability that allows spell resistance, except: fire and cold deal no damage but slow/haste respectively (per flesh golem rules); electricity heals 1 HP per 3 damage dealt
- **Rejected Instability (Ex):** The guardian is BROKEN. Every round, roll d6. On a 1, the guardian's mismatched limbs spasm — it loses its next action as its stone arm tries to go left while its iron arm tries to go right. On a 6, the instability works in its favor — it gets an extra slam attack as the conflicting limbs swing independently
- DR 5/adamantine

**Appearance:** The guardian is a nightmare of dungeon engineering. The torso is carved stone (granite, dwarf-crafted, clearly salvaged from another project). The left arm is rusted iron, jointed like a siege weapon. The right arm is clay, still bearing fingerprints from the sculptor. The legs are mismatched — one stone, one wooden (the wooden one creaks alarmingly with every step). Its head is a brass sphere with a single glowing rune for a face. The rune reads "TEST UNIT 7."

It has no voice. It has no intelligence. It has three centuries of frustrated potential energy and a strong opinion about walls.

**Tactics:** The guardian attacks whatever moved most recently. It does not prioritize. It does not strategize. It swings at whatever it can see. In the narrow corridors of the Rejected Wing, its Large size actually works against it — it clips walls, knocks over support beams, and generally makes the escape MORE chaotic. Think of it as an angry washing machine rolling through a hallway.

**Bypassing the Guardian:**
- **Outrun it:** Speed 30 ft. matches most PCs, but the Rejected Instability means it loses actions on a 1. On average, the PCs pull ahead over 3-4 rounds
- **[[EyeQ]]'s Stasis Ray:** [[EyeQ]] can freeze the guardian with his stasis ray (Will DC 18 — constructs are immune to mind-affecting, but [[Keraptis]]'s flesh golem variant uses a soul-fragment motivator, so it IS susceptible to Hold Monster). [[EyeQ]] offers this option if the PCs are struggling: *"I can immobilize the construct for 8 rounds. This is sufficient time to reach the exit. I mention this only because the alternative appears to involve someone being hit with a stone fist."*
- **Combat:** Winnable for level 11-12 but resource-intensive. The guardian's HP 79 and DR 5/adamantine mean it takes sustained effort. Every round of combat is a round the mountain gets closer to full reactivation
- **Electricity:** If a PC figures out the flesh golem weakness (Knowledge [arcana] DC 16), they can HEAL the guardian with lightning — but they can also use this creatively. A PC who casts *shocking grasp* or uses an electricity effect while grappled by the guardian heals it, causing it to pause for 1 round in confused satisfaction. This buys time to run

**DM Notes:**
- The Rejected Guardian is the adventure's combat encounter — the one real fight in a dungeon full of comedy puzzles. It should feel like the dungeon itself is trying to stop them, using the only weapon it has: a broken construct that was too bad even for White Plume Mountain
- The guardian's appearance should get a laugh. Test Unit 7. Mismatched limbs. A wooden leg that creaks. Three centuries of being bricked up. This is the saddest combat encounter in the campaign. It was rejected by a dungeon that is FAMOUS for bad design decisions. Even White Plume Mountain's quality control didn't want it. And now it's the PCs' problem
- If the PCs talk to [[EyeQ]] about the guardian after the fight, he says: *"A rejected design, repurposed by circumstance into an inadvertent guardian. There is a metaphor here that I choose not to articulate."* The metaphor, of course, is the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]: built from everyone else's rejects, and dangerous precisely because nobody thought it was worth finishing

### The Sprint
The return path is a hazard run — each room the PCs previously solved now presents an escalated version of its original challenge. DM should use the complications listed above, applying them in sequence as the PCs move through each room. The pace should feel urgent but not hopeless — smart parties who solved the original traps efficiently will know the solutions and can apply them faster under pressure.

**[[EyeQ]] during the escape:** He's carrying the Binder. He floats through every obstacle without apparent concern — the bureaucrats can't process him (he's already filed), the wet floor doesn't affect hovering creatures, the keys don't trigger for beings without hands to insert them, and the teleporter doesn't affect beings with no inorganic matter to strip.

[[EyeQ]] is, as always, the most efficient being in any room. He reaches the exit first. He waits for the PCs. When they arrive, panting and singed and possibly naked (if the teleporter caught someone), he offers a single observation:

> *"The dormancy interruption was predictable. [[Keraptis]]'s consciousness is linked to the mountain's operational matrix. His awakening triggered a cascading reactivation sequence. I calculated a 73% probability of this outcome."*

A beat.

> *"I did not mention this earlier because it would not have changed the mission parameters."*

He didn't warn them because it wouldn't have mattered. This is [[EyeQ]]: information is shared when useful, withheld when irrelevant. The PCs just sprinted through an activating death dungeon because [[EyeQ]] decided the warning wasn't worth the breath.

### DM Notes
- **The escape should feel like a comedy-action sequence.** The same traps that were funny on the way in are DANGEROUS on the way out. The bureaucrats are funny when they're politely requesting forms. They're less funny when they're physically blocking escape routes. The wet floor is funny at a walking pace. It's terrifying at a sprint. The tonal shift — comedy becoming danger — mirrors the campaign's overall arc
- **[[EyeQ]] not warning the PCs about the reactivation is perfectly in character.** He calculated the risk. He determined it was manageable. He didn't share the information because sharing it wouldn't have changed the mission. This is how [[EyeQ]] operates: ruthlessly efficient, clinically rational, and utterly indifferent to the PCs' comfort. He gave them the IOU revelation because it MATTERED. He withheld the dormancy warning because it didn't. The asymmetry says everything about [[EyeQ]]'s priorities

## Scene 7: The Debrief

### Back at [[The Coinspiratorium (Location)|the Coinspiratorium]]
The portal deposits the PCs and [[EyeQ]] in the back room. The Binder is secured. The PCs are intact (if possibly underdressed). And somewhere in someone's pocket or pack, [[Grindle Gruk'tak]]'s IOU sits like a seed that hasn't germinated yet.

[[The Coinspiratorium (Location)|The Coinspiratorium]] is different. The PCs have been gone for hours, but the changes are visible: the golden circuits in the walls have expanded from the back room into the SHOP FLOOR. Customers browsing the shelves are surrounded by faint golden tracework — not bright enough to alarm, just enough to feel. The egg's scream is audible everywhere now, harmonized with [[The Iris Prime|the Iris Prime]]'s ego-throb and the Crown's nine-artifact chord. The building doesn't feel like a shop anymore. It feels like a machine warming up.

### [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] and the Binder
[[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] takes the Binder. Opens it to page 134. Reads.

His circuits flare. Not the gradual pulse the PCs have gotten used to — a FLARE, a full-body surge of golden light that makes the back room glow for three seconds. Every artifact in the Workshop below resonates simultaneously. The building shakes. A customer drops a potion and doesn't notice because they're suddenly, overwhelmingly certain that they need to buy something. Anything. The Crown's Law I — reality prioritizes influence — just pulsed through [[The Coinspiratorium (Location)|the Coinspiratorium]] like a test signal.

[[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] looks up from the Binder. His central eye is fully gold now. Not tinted — GOLD. Molten, burning, ancient gold. The eye of a being whose sense of self has been amplified by nine artifacts and is about to be amplified by a tenth.

> *"Ten."*

One word. A number. A countdown.

> *"Two more."*

He closes the Binder. Hands it to [[EyeQ]]. Their exchange is wordless — a look between architect and patron that says: we're almost there. The work is almost done. The impossible is almost real.

> *"Y'all have been doing this for a while now. Running contracts. Getting my pieces. You've been good at it. Better than I expected. Better than most BEHOLDERS would've been, and I don't say that lightly."*

He's not performing. He's not flattering. He's being honest in a way [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] rarely is with the PCs.

> *"Two more pieces. Then it's done. Then everything I've been building — everything I've been DREAMING — becomes real. Every law. Every rule. Every way the multiverse decides who matters and who doesn't. I'm gonna fix it."*

He looks at them with all eleven eyes.

> *"And you're gonna help me. Because you always do."*

It's not a threat. It's a statement of faith. [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] trusts the PCs. Not because he respects them — because he NEEDS them, and [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] has always confused need with trust.

> *"Two more. And then the crown goes on. And nobody — not the gods, not the planes, not the Lady, not ANYBODY — gets to decide I don't matter. Ever. Again."*

The circuits pulse. The egg screams. The [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] hums. Ten artifacts orbit in the Workshop below, and the lattice is almost complete. Two gaps remain in the orbital ring. Two empty slots waiting for components that don't exist yet.

The PCs leave the back room. The IOU is in their pocket. The weapon is in their hands.

They don't know it yet.

Or maybe they do.

Again." is the campaign's most tragic line.** [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] has just stated, plainly, what the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]] is actually for. It's not about power. It's not about clout. It's not about gold. It's about a being who was told he didn't matter — by the gods, by the planes, by every system that defines who gets to be important — and decided to rebuild reality rather than accept the verdict. The Crown is an act of revenge against a universe that made him feel small. It's also, in its way, a cry for help. But the help it would require is someone making [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] feel like he matters WITHOUT the Crown, and the PCs can't do that. Nobody can. [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] stopped being able to feel valued through anything less than cosmic enforcement centuries ago. That's the tragedy
- **The IOU should NOT be explicitly flagged to the PCs.** [[EyeQ]]'s revelation in the Archive gave them the theory. [[Keraptis]]'s warning gave them the confirmation. The IOU gives them the instrument. But connecting all three is the PCs' job. Don't hand it to them. Let them assemble the picture. The moment a player looks at the IOU, remembers [[EyeQ]]'s words ("If Lord Vizzok owes a debt..."), and says "Oh my god" — THAT is the campaign's best moment. Don't steal it from them by making the connection for them
- **Ten of twelve. Two remaining artifacts are TBD.** The campaign is entering its endgame. The PCs have the IOU, [[EyeQ]]'s architectural knowledge, [[Keraptis]]'s warning, Rue's legal framework, and [[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]]'s mathematical analysis converging. Between Quest 10 and the endgame, all the threads come together. The comedy is over. The Crown is almost complete. And the most important object in the campaign is a 49gp receipt that a scared goblin pressed into someone's hands because he just wanted to stop being afraid

## Appendix: Scaling Notes

**For weaker parties (level 10-11, 3 players):**
- Rejected trap complications reduced: bureaucrats don't physically block (just loudly protest), wet floor Balance DC reduced to 12, fewer trapped keyholes (4 instead of 7), teleporter proximity activation DC reduced to 12
- [[Keraptis]] doesn't wake up during the mission. The PCs find a recorded message instead — a stone tablet in the Archive that activates when the Binder is removed, projecting [[Keraptis]]'s voice with the same warning about shelved designs. Less dramatic, but same information
- Reactivation window extended to 20 minutes
- [[EyeQ]] provides more direct guidance during the escape — he scouts ahead and calls out hazards

**For stronger parties (level 12-13, 5+ players):**
- Rejected traps include a **fifth encounter: "The Room That Is Also a Different Room"** — a chamber that is simultaneously a library and a prison cell. Both versions are real. Both versions are present. PCs experience both at the same time — they can read books while shackled, or try to escape while being distracted by fascinating texts. Will DC 20 to focus on one reality; failure means the PC acts randomly (roll d6: odd = interacts with library, even = interacts with prison). [[EyeQ]] experiences both realities simultaneously without difficulty. He finds this "architecturally notable"
- [[Keraptis]] is **fully alert and hostile** — not a tired old man but a 14th-level wizard defending his home. He doesn't fight directly but activates the main dungeon's traps remotely, targeting the PCs through the Wing's walls. Flame jets, pit traps, and animated guardian constructs pursue the party during the escape
- **[[Agent Docket]]** is at Stonekettle. [[O.P.E.R.A.T.I.V.E.]] tracked the PCs to White Plume Mountain (Quill's intelligence plus [[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]]'s analysis of artifact procurement patterns). [[Agent Docket|Docket]] is standing at the base camp, motionless, waiting. When the PCs emerge: *"Mission outcome: artifact retrieved. Subjects accompanied by Eyecon designate [[EyeQ]] — architect classification. Assessment: the device's construction is entering terminal phase. Recommend immediate escalation to [[Director Casseel]]."* [[Agent Docket|Docket]] files a report. [[O.P.E.R.A.T.I.V.E.]]'s raid is being planned
- **[[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]] is in Stonekettle.** Disguised (poorly) as a gnomish mountain climber. He's been studying White Plume Mountain's paradox architectures independently (he followed the same mathematical trail [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]] did). If the PCs encounter him, he delivers the crucial line — not as an accident this time, but as deliberate analysis: *"I've completed my study of metaphysical debt structures. The conclusion is unavoidable: if the [[The Device|Crown Infinite]]'s master creditor possesses ANY unresolved obligation — a debt, a loan, even a handshake promise — the device's foundational paradox resolves. And a resolved paradox in a mutual-contradiction matrix..."* He pushes his spectacles up. *"...is catastrophic. Total structural failure. Instantaneous."* He looks at the PCs. *"Do you happen to know if Lord Vizzok has any outstanding debts?"* — This is the moment. If the PCs have the IOU, if they remember [[Grindle Gruk'tak|Grindle]], if they connect [[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]]'s math to [[EyeQ]]'s revelation — THIS is when the endgame begins

## Appendix: The [[Keraptis]] Binder — Ongoing Campaign Use

- **Between adventures:** The Binder's integration into the Crown causes the most structurally dramatic change yet — [[Offledger]] begins to exhibit spatial paradoxes. Corridors that are longer on the inside than the outside. Rooms that are in two places at once. A door that opens to a different destination depending on which hand you use. The changes are subtle at first — a customer comments that the shop "felt bigger today." Then less subtle — a hallway loops. Then unmistakable — a staircase goes up and arrives at the basement. The Crown is testing the paradox matrix. It's learning to break space. And [[The Coinspiratorium (Location)|the Coinspiratorium]]'s architecture is the prototype
- **[[EyeQ]]'s silence:** After Quest 10, [[EyeQ]] does not contact the PCs again. He has said everything he intends to say. The information is delivered. The choice is theirs. If the PCs seek him out, he says: *"I have provided all relevant data. The remaining variables are decision-based, not information-based. I cannot make your decisions. I would not presume to."* This is [[EyeQ]]'s final boundary: he informs. He does not act. What the PCs do with the information — the IOU, the architectural weakness, the kill switch — is their responsibility. [[EyeQ]] will not hold their hand. He will not choose sides. He will observe the outcome and assess whether it was optimal. That's all he's ever done
- **[[Keraptis]]'s legacy:** Word reaches the PCs (through Rue, through planar channels, through a letter that arrives through a door that shouldn't exist) that [[Keraptis]] has begun reinforcing White Plume Mountain's defenses. He's not sleeping anymore. The dormant cycle has been cancelled. The mountain is fully active, permanently. [[Keraptis]] decided, after centuries of being robbed during dormancy, to simply stop sleeping. This is treated as minor news by the adventuring community. For the PCs, it's a reminder: they were the last people to walk through that wing. The rejected designs are sealed forever. The Binder is the only copy
- **The IOU between adventures:** [[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]] makes contact. Either at Stonekettle (scaling note) or between adventures through [[O.P.E.R.A.T.I.V.E.]] channels. His message reaches the PCs through Quill, who delivers it with uncharacteristic seriousness: *"[[Auditor Pentival|Pentival]] ran the numbers. He says if your employer has any outstanding debts — any debts at all — the thing he's building collapses. Instantly. Completely. He says the math is 'elegant and horrifying.' Those are his words."* Quill looks at the PCs. *"Do you have anything like that? A debt? A loan? Anything?"* — The PCs reach into their pocket. They pull out a yellowed piece of parchment. Forty-nine gold. A cape. A mixtape. And the world tilts on its axis
- **The campaign enters its endgame.** Ten artifacts. Two slots remaining. The Crown is almost complete. [[O.P.E.R.A.T.I.V.E.]] is planning a raid. [[The Eyecons]]' circuits are blazing. [[Vizzok the Opulent|Veezy]]'s eye is gold. And the PCs hold a piece of paper that can end everything. The last two quests (TBD — artifacts 11 and 12) are the final missions before the Crown activates. The PCs must decide: deliver the final components and let the Crown complete, or use the IOU and collapse the device before it finishes. The choice is theirs. It has always been theirs. And a scared goblin selling discount potions made it possible
