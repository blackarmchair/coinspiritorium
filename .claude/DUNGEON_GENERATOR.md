# Purpose

Defines a repeatable structure for generating **Coinspiratorium-style dungeons**.

Dungeons in this campaign are **thematic artifact vaults**, not generic monster caves.

---

# Exploration System

All dungeons should be designed with the **Dungeon Exploration System** in mind (see `Rules/Dungeon Exploration.md`). This means:

* Distances between rooms matter (movement costs turns)
* Light conditions and duration affect resource pressure
* Noise-generating obstacles create encounter risk tradeoffs
* Environmental timers (flooding, collapse, alarms) add macro-urgency
* Room density and searchable content create thoroughness-vs-speed decisions

When generating dungeons, consider how the turn structure will interact with the layout. Dead ends cost time. Long corridors burn movement. Locked doors and traps force the party to spend turns, and every turn is a roll on the encounter clock.

# Spatial Design: Jaquaysing the Dungeon

Dungeons should be **nonlinear labyrinths**, not fancy-looking corridors with combat in between. The following principles (derived from Jennell Jaquays' dungeon design philosophy, as popularized by Justin Alexander) define how Coinspiratorium dungeons should be structured spatially.

The core idea: **if everything is on the same path, you're not making exploration choices.** Variable paths through a space create meaningful, informed decisions. Every dungeon layout should present the party with real navigational options.

## Map Loops

The most essential structural element. Dungeons should be **cyclical**, not linear.

Loops let you build mazes with hubs. A central room that the party keeps returning to helps them orient themselves, while the looping paths create a satisfying feeling of comprehension as players gradually map their surroundings. Think of recognizing a landmark on an unfamiliar drive and realizing you know exactly where you are.

A good hub room offers multiple exits in different directions. Five or more exits from a single hub gives the party genuine choice about where to explore next. At least some of those paths should loop back, so the party knows that poking around won't strand them.

**When generating dungeon layouts, default to loops. Linear sequences of rooms should be the exception, used deliberately (e.g., the final approach to a boss encounter) rather than out of habit.**

## Level Loops

Dungeons with multiple levels should not be "two dungeons back to back." Moving between floors is part of the exploration, not something you do once you've cleared a floor.

* **Multiple connections between levels.** Not just one stairway down. Stairways, ladders, holes, collapsed floors, elevator platforms, teleportation circles. Different paths to different parts of the next level.
* **Mid-entry.** The party doesn't have to start on floor one. They could enter on a middle level with paths going up and down.
* **Discontinuous levels.** Floor 3 might connect to both floor 4 and floor 5, but floor 4 has no path to floor 5. The party has to backtrack and find the other route. This increases the labyrinth feeling without being unfair.
* **Minor elevation shifts.** Stairways up and down on a single floor that don't lead to a new level, just provide elevation variation within the current one.

## Multiple Entrances

Build several ways into the dungeon. These entrances become exits at later points, rewarding exploration both inside and outside the dungeon. They also serve as escape routes.

Consider varied entrance types: a front door, a hidden side entrance, a dangerous shortcut straight to a late-game area. When players see a big pit leading into the boss chamber, they can choose to take it and skip the dungeon's loot, or explore properly and arrive prepared. Jaquaysing is about presenting those kinds of choices.

## Strange Paths

Hidden or unusual connections between areas. Secret doors into long corridors. Traps that drop into forgotten chambers. Cracks in walls, holes between floors, viewing platforms overlooking other rooms.

These don't have to be tunnels with ladders. A gap you can misty step across, a drainage pipe you can crawl through, an observation deck looking down into a room two floors below. Strange paths create the feeling of discovery that makes exploration rewarding. You found a path. Where does it lead?

## Sub-Levels and Divided Dungeons

Play with the dungeon's overall shape:

* **Sub-levels:** Between floor 1 and floor 2, a small self-contained area (floor 1A) with its own content. A dead-end pocket worth exploring.
* **Divided dungeons:** Two separate complexes connected by limited paths. Maybe the dungeon is actually two structures that merged, or two towers with bridges between their floors.
* **Planar connections:** Dungeons in this campaign can (and should) cross into other planes, demiplanes, or realities. A portal in a wall leading to a completely different environment. A room that exists simultaneously in two locations. This is especially appropriate for Coinspiratorium dungeons, which are often built by powerful entities with access to planar magic. Use dungeon space as an excuse to visit somewhere unexpected, even briefly.

## Applying These Principles

Not every dungeon needs every technique. These are tools to reach for when appropriate:

* A short dungeon might only need one good loop and a strange path
* A large dungeon should have a clear hub, multiple level connections, and at least one alternate entrance
* Linear sequences are fine when used deliberately (a final corridor to the boss, a trapped gauntlet)
* The turn-based exploration system amplifies all of this: loops mean the party weighs "explore the new branch or return to the hub?" against their remaining torch time and encounter clock pressure

**The goal is that the party makes meaningful navigation choices throughout the dungeon, not just combat choices in a predetermined sequence of rooms.**

# Random Encounter Tables: Inspiring, Not Dictating

Random encounter tables for dungeons (and travel) should be **inspiring prompts**, not rigid stat blocks. The goal is a table where every result excites the DM, and no roll wastes the table's time answering an obvious question ("Will our 6th-level party die to 10 wolves?" No. Next.)

This approach is drawn from Davi of Mystic Arts, building on principles from Justin Alexander and classic encounter design.

## The Core Problem

A table that says "3d6 wolves" is too rigid. It doesn't account for pacing, party level, or the mood at the table. If the party is high-level, the wolves are a waste of time. If the party just finished a grueling combat, another fight might be the wrong call. And if the result is boring, rolling for it just makes the boredom feel mandatory.

A table that's entirely authored (the DM picks every encounter) loses the generative quality that makes the world feel real. Rolling on a table creates the feeling of peeking into the world and seeing what's happening. Pure authorship makes everything feel arbitrary.

**The solution: do both at the same time.** The table provides randomized content. The DM authors the execution.

## Each Entry Is a Theme, Not a Stat Block

Every entry on the table should be a **theme** with built-in variations. Write each entry with three components:

1. **Meat and potatoes.** The core encounter. Wolves. Undead. A patrol. Whatever it is at baseline.
2. **Special variations.** Harder, stranger, or more interesting versions. Direwolves, a werewolf pack leader, a dire bear traveling with the pack. Options the DM can reach for when the moment calls for escalation.
3. **Non-combat option.** An alternative that doesn't require initiative. A wounded wolf pup the party could adopt. Wolf tracks leading to a lair. A dead wolf killed by something else on the table. This gives the DM an exit ramp when combat would kill the pacing.

Example entry:

> **Wolfpack.** A wolfpack circles the camp and charges if they think they can take the party. Each beast bears the signed mark of Strahd on their paws. The pack consists mostly of wolves, but might include direwolves or a werewolf pack leader. For high-level parties, consider a dire bear. Alternatively, the party might find a single wounded wolf pup. A DC 18 Animal Handling check and a ration could endear the wolf to a character as a potential sidekick.

That single entry covers five different encounters depending on what the moment needs.

## No Boring Results

If a result is boring, it will inevitably come up. The solution is to not have boring results. Every entry should make the DM want to run it.

A grave with rusted armor is not an encounter. It involves no choices, no investigation, nothing. A grave where a DC 15 Investigation check reveals a map to a nearby dungeon, letters from a villain, an uncorroded magic item, or a full coin purse is an encounter. It rewards curiosity and good rolls. It has gameplay.

**Every entry should contain at least one informed choice for the players.** If there's no decision to make, it's set dressing, not an encounter.

## Prep DM vs. Running DM

Think of encounter design as a collaboration between two versions of yourself:

* **Prep DM** knows the world, the lore, the themes. They build the table with rich, varied entries. They decide what *could* happen.
* **Running DM** knows the pacing, the mood, the party's current state. They look at what Prep DM wrote and decide what *should* happen right now.

Prep DM creates the options. Running DM picks the right one. Neither works alone. Prep DM trusts Running DM to adapt in the moment. Running DM trusts Prep DM to have stocked the table with good material.

When building tables, **do all the prep work but none of the planning.** Fill the table with exciting, flexible content. Then trust yourself to deploy it well at the table.

## Use Bell Curves, Not Flat Distributions

Use 2d6 (or similar multi-die rolls) instead of a single d20 or d12. Multiple dice create a bell curve where common results cluster in the middle and rare results sit at the extremes. This is useful because:

* Common encounters (loot, environmental features) go in the middle where they'll come up most often
* Rare encounters (major NPCs, boss-level threats) go at the extremes
* The probability distribution does design work for you: you don't have to manually assign percentages

A 2d6 table gives a 16% chance of rolling 7 and under a 3% chance of rolling 2 or 12. That weighting is built into the dice. You don't have to think about it.

## Authored vs. Random: When to Use Each

Not every encounter needs to be rolled for. Sometimes you know it's time to reintroduce a plot hook or foreshadow something. Just do it. That's setup, not gameplay.

The distinction:

* **Setup encounters** are authored. You decide the party finds footprints with a chain dragging behind them because you want to foreshadow the revenant. No dice involved.
* **Gameplay encounters** are rolled. The dice tell you wolves, and you decide whether that means a pack, a werewolf, or a wounded pup based on the current mood.

Both are valid. Alternate between them. If you want to disguise the difference, roll dice during setup encounters and ignore the result. The players don't need to know which encounters were authored and which were generated.

## Applying This to Coinspiratorium Dungeons

The turn-based exploration system already creates an encounter clock. Every turn spent in a dungeon risks triggering a random encounter. The tables for those encounters should follow these principles:

* Each dungeon's encounter table should reflect its theme. A celestial archive has different encounters than a cursed theater.
* Entries should include non-combat options that interact with the dungeon's unique mechanics or lore.
* The bell curve should weight common encounters (patrols, environmental hazards, minor discoveries) in the middle and rare encounters (the dungeon's guardian, a fragment of the artifact's power, a planar bleed-through) at the extremes.
* Every entry should make the party's resource-management decisions harder, not just drain hit points. An encounter that forces a difficult choice (spend a spell slot or lose time, make noise or take a longer path) is better than one that just deals damage.

# Dungeon Generation Framework

Generate the following elements.

```
Dungeon Theme
Original Owner
Reason It Exists
Artifact Stored Inside
Environmental Identity
Guardians
Unique Mechanics
Dungeon Twist
```

---

# Dungeon Themes

Themes should be strong and unusual.

Examples:

* dragon hoard vault
* celestial archive
* cursed theater
* time-loop prison
* planar museum
* magical casino

Avoid generic fantasy ruins.

---

# Original Owner

Every dungeon once belonged to someone.

Examples:

* archmage
* dragon
* celestial order
* lich collector
* ancient empire

This determines the dungeon style.

---

# Reason It Exists

Why was this place created?

Examples:

* vault for powerful artifacts
* prison for magical beings
* research facility
* divine trial chamber

---

# Artifact

Every dungeon revolves around an artifact.

Artifacts should:

* be narratively significant
* have unusual magical properties
* be desirable to Veezy

---

# Environmental Identity

Give the dungeon a strong visual identity.

Examples:

* floating rooms connected by chains
* endless library corridors
* volcanic caverns
* rooms filled with water

---

# Guardians

Guardians should match the theme.

Examples:

* constructs
* summoned outsiders
* magical traps
* cursed spirits

---

# Unique Mechanics

Each dungeon should contain a **distinct mechanic**.

Examples:

* rooms rearrange themselves
* gravity changes direction
* illusions distort reality
* magical contracts bind players

---

# Dungeon Twist

The dungeon should contain a narrative surprise.

Examples:

* the artifact is sentient
* the dungeon itself is alive
* removing the artifact triggers catastrophe
* the artifact is not what it appears

---

# Example Dungeon

Theme: Celestial Museum

Original Owner: Solar archivists

Reason: Preserve divine relics

Artifact: Halo of the First Dawn

Environment:

* marble halls
* floating display cases
* radiant light everywhere

Guardians:

* animated statues
* angelic constructs

Mechanic:

* relics test the morality of intruders

Twist:

* the halo removes free will from its wearer
